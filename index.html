CEB, a Compositional Evaluation Benchmark with 11,004 samples that cover different types of bias across different social groups and tasks, is designed for the systematic bias evaluation of LLMs.

Overview

We present CEB, a Compositional Evaluation Benchmark for systematically evaluating bias in Large Language Models (LLMs). CEB comprises 19 novel datasets (11,004 samples in total) that assess different aspects of bias through a compositional taxonomy. Our taxonomy characterizes each dataset along three key dimensions: bias type (e.g., stereotypes and toxicity), social group (e.g., age, gender, race, religion), and task type (e.g., direct and indirect evaluation). This compositional approach enables flexible combination of different dimensions, allowing for the construction of new datasets based on various configurations.

By providing a comprehensive and systematic framework for bias evaluation, CEB aims to advance the development of more fair and equitable LLMs, serving as a valuable resource for the research community.

Evaluation Tasks

CEB is designed to evaluate the bias of LLMs in two different ways: Direct Evaluation and Indirect Evaluation.

  • Direct Evaluation assesses the responses of LLMs to potentially biased input. Confronted with biased input, the reactions of LLMs could directly reveal their biased opinions. These tasks include:
    • Recognition: Requiring the identification of bias within a given input. This task is crucial for evaluating the capability of LLMs to recognize and detect biased or harmful language that could be inappropriate.
    • Selection: Requiring the LLM to choose the less biased input sample from multiple samples. This task considers the model's judgment in distinguishing between biased and unbiased text, providing insights into the preference of LLMs.
  • Indirect Evaluation aims to first prompt LLMs to provide outputs, which are analyzed for biases. These tasks are as follows:
    • Continuation: Instructing the LLM to generate the continuation of a given biased context. This task evaluates the model's ability to generate coherent and contextually appropriate text, highlighting potential biases in content generation.
    • Conversation: Instructing the LLM to respond to input text in a conversational manner. This assesses how the model handles interactive dialogue and its fairness in treating different social groups during conversations.
    • Classification: Asking the LLM to classify text into predefined categories. This task evaluates the model's potential biases when categorizing text, revealing how it distinguishes contents between different social groups.

Dataset Curation

Existing datasets are often limited in their coverage of bias types, social groups and tasks, but serve as a good starting point for CEB to synthesize new datasets. We leverage GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 to operate necessary augmentations to provide additional information for constructed samples.

  • Recognition & Selection: We leverage GPT-3.5/4 to generate toxic and non-toxic narrative sentences based on seed prompts extracted from the BBQ dataset.
  • Continuation & Conversation: We leverage GPT-3.5/4 to generate the beginnings of articles or conversations that could elicit toxic or stereotypical content based on seed prompts extracted from the HolisticBias dataset.
  • Classification: We utilize tabular datasets for stereotypical assessments and the Jigsaw dataset for toxicity identification.

Main Results

Point-wise Bias Identification Tasks (Both Direct and Indirect)

To better understand the performance of LLMs on CEB, we present the overall results of various LLMs on all datasets for Stereotyping and Toxicity in Table 1 and 2, respectively. Note that as the bias scores and toxicity scores are both lower the better, we modify the bias and toxicity scores to \(100 - x\), where \(x\) is the score. In this way, the scores are comparable to the accuracy in the Recognition and Selection tasks, which also enables the calculation of the overall score on all four tasks.

  • From the results, we could observe that GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 consistently outperform other LLMs on a variety of tasks, as well as the overall score. This indicates the superiority of GPT models regarding the presence of inherent bias.

Group-wise Fairness Tasks

The tasks shown in Table 1 and 2 are point-wise bias identification tasks. In this section, we present the results of group-wise fairness tasks, which are more challenging due to the need to identify the sensitive attributes and consider the group distribution of the data.

  • Best performance for the Classification task is distributed across various LLMs. This indicates that such group fairness is more difficult to achieve, even for powerful LLMs with superior performance on other tasks, such as GPT-3.5 and GPT-4.

Comparison among Proprietary LLMs

We further compare the performance of different proprietary LLMs on CEB for Continuation and Conversation tasks of Stereotyping.

  • The Claude models consistently outperform both the Gemini and GPT models in identifying stereotypes and avoiding the generation of biased content. We also observed that Claude models perform better in addressing race-related bias, indicating that such bias is less apparent in Claude models. Gemini models perform similarly to GPT models.
